# UX: MCQ + Short Q
## What are the primary goals of UX design?
- To understand users’ needs, behaviors, and motivations and design products that ==**effectively meet those needs**==
- To employ various techniques, tools, and methodologies to create ==**intuitive and user-centered designs**==

> ### Purpose of UX Design: Creating Meaningful Experiences
> UX Design is not just a trend; it's a fundamental aspect of creating successful products and services in the digital age. By focusing on the user, we can create experiences that are not only functional but also enjoyable and meaningful.
> - is all about ==**crafting positive and meaningful experiences for users**== when they interact with a product or service
> - it's about ==**understanding user needs, behaviours, and motivations to create solutions that are useful, usable, and desirable**==
> - good UX design would make the process easy and intuitive, guiding you through each step seamlessly
> - bad UX design, on the other hand, might leave you confused, frustrated, and ultimately abandoning your booking
> - UX design aims to prevent these negative experiences
> ### Benefits of Good UX
> - ==**Increased User Satisfaction**==
> 	- Happy users are more likely to return and recommend your product.
> - ==**Improved Usability**==
> 	- Easy-to-use products lead to higher efficiency and fewer errors.
>- ==**Higher Conversion Rates**==
>	- A smooth user experience can lead to more sales or desired actions.
>- ==**Positive Brand Perception**==
>	- A positive experience reflects well on your brand and builds trust.
>- ==**Reduced Development Costs**==
>	- Addressing UX issues early in the design process can save time and money later.

## What are some common IA structures?
- ==**Hierarchical**==
	- ==**Tree-like structure**==, with a main category branching into subcategories (e.g., a typical e-commerce website).
- ==**Sequential**==
	- ==**Linear flow**==, guiding users through a specific process (e.g., an online checkout process).
- ==**Matrix**==
	- Content organized by ==**multiple categories, allowing users to explore in different ways**== (e.g., a recipe website with filters for cuisine, ingredients, and dietary restrictions).

## What is the primary focus of User-Centred Design (UCD)?
- ==**Prioritizing the needs and preferences of the end user**== throughout the design process

> By adhering to these principles, designers can create products and services that are not only functional but also enjoyable and meaningful for the user.
> - ==**Focus on the user**==
> 	- Understand ==**user needs, goals, and limitations**== at every stage of the design process.
> - ==**Empathy**==
> 	- Put ==**yourself in the user's shoes**== and understand their perspective.
> - ==**Iteration**==
> 	- Design, test, and refine your designs based on user feedback. UCD is an iterative process.
> - ==**Testing**==
> 	- Regularly test your designs with users to identify usability issues and ensure that the product meets their needs.

> ### Designing for Accessibility: Inclusive Design
> By designing inclusively, we can create products and services that are usable and enjoyable by everyone, regardless of their abilities.
> - WCAG Guidelines
> 	- Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are a set of international standards for making web content accessible.
> - Screen Readers
> 	- Software that reads aloud the content of a website or app for users with visual impairments. (aria attributes)
> - ==**Color Contrast**==
> 	- Sufficient contrast between text and background
> 	- right **color contrast** must always be identified
> 	- color should never be the only means to convey information
> 	- should be at least a **4.5 : 1 ratio** between the foreground content and the background content

> ### Designing for Accessibility: Inclusive Design
> - ==**Keyboard Navigation**==
> 	- Users who cannot use a mouse should be able to navigate a website using the keyboard.
> - ==**Alternative Text for Images**==
> 	- Descriptive text for images allows screen readers to convey the image content to users with visual impairments.
> - ==**Captions and Transcripts for Videos**==
> 	- Provide captions for users who are deaf or hard of hearing, and transcripts for users who prefer to read the content.

### UX Design vs. UI Design: The Dynamic Duo
| Feature          | UX Design (User Experience)                                     | UI Design (User Interface)                           |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Focus**        | ==**User needs, usability, overall experience**==               | Visual design, aesthetics, interaction elements      |
| **Goal**         | Create a ==**positive and meaningful experience**==             | Create a visually appealing and functional interface |
| **Deliverables** | User research, wireframes, prototypes, information architecture | Mockups, style guides, design specifications         |
| **Example**      | Ensuring a website is easy to navigate                          | Choosing the right colors and fonts for the website  |
- UX and UI work together seamlessly. A great UI enhances a good UX, and vice versa
	- beautiful interface won't be effective if it's not user-friendly, and a highly usable product might not be appealing if it looks outdated!

### User Research Methods: Understanding the User's Voice
The choice of research method depends on the project goals, budget, and timeline. Often, a combination of qualitative and quantitative methods is used to get a complete picture of the user.
- about understanding their needs, behaviors, and motivations through user research
- research informs design decisions and ensures that the final product meets user expectations
### Qualitative Research Methods
- understanding user behavior and motivations
- User Interviews
	- One-on-one conversations with users to gather in-depth insights.
	- Ask open-ended questions like: _"Tell me about your experience with using this coffee ordering app."_
- Usability Testing
	- Observing users as they interact with a prototype or existing product to identify usability issues.
	- _Focus on observing user behavior, not just asking for opinions._
- Contextual Inquiry
	- Observing users in their natural environment as they use a product or service.
	- _Observing how commuters use the app to order coffee._
- Surveys (Open-ended questions)
	- Gathering feedback from a larger group of users, focusing on qualitative insights.
### Quantitative Methods
- collecting numerical data and identifying trends
- Surveys (Closed-ended questions)
	- Gathering quantifiable data from a large sample size.
	- _On a scale of 1 to 5, how satisfied are you with coffee ordering app?_
- A/B Testing
	- Comparing two versions of a design to see which performs better.
	- _Testing two different button colors to see which leads to more clicks._
- Analytics
	- Tracking user behavior on a website or app to understand how users interact with the product.
	- _Analyzing website traffic to see which pages are most popular._
### Elements of a Persona
- Demographics
	- Age, location, occupation, education.
	- _If it is a local app, use Singaporean personas._
- Goals
	- What the user wants to achieve.
	- _Order a quick and affordable meal._
- Needs
	- What the user requires to achieve their goals.
	- _Easy navigation, secure payment options, delivery tracking._
- Frustrations (Pain Points)
	- Pain points the user experiences.
	- _Long wait times, inaccurate delivery estimates, limited payment options._
- Motivations
	- What drives the user's behavior.
	- _Convenience, value for money, healthy eating._
- Quote
	- A short statement that captures the user's personality or goals.
### Simplified UCD Process: ADEI (aligned to IBM SkillsBuild)
![[Pasted image 20260508222938.jpg|600]]
- typically involves several iterative steps so that the final product or service meets the needs and expectations of the users
- The UCD process is not always linear. Often, designers will move back and forth between different stages as they learn more about the users and refine their designs.
- Analyze
	- start by learning about the individuals who’ll use what they’re creating
	- analyze what users want and need, their pain points and preferences, and how they’ll use the product
	- ensures the subsequent design and development of the product is based on insights and not assumptions
	- is a crucial guide for UX designers because it helps them create user-centered designs
- Design
	- initial concepts, drawings, and prototypes, which are early versions of what they’re creating
	- a collaborative and iterative process where the UX designers work together with the developers and other stakeholders
	- use what they learned during the analyze step to make sure the user interface and experience meet user expectations and goals
- Evaluate
	- let real users try out and test what they’ve designed for the product, system, or service
	- Individuals use it and provide feedback, sharing what’s working well and what needs improvement
	- helps in assessing the effectiveness of the design in meeting the user’s needs and enhancing the overall experience
	- use various methods and techniques to evaluate the usability of the design solution and determine if it’s aligning with the project goals and objectives
- Implement
	- turning the refined design into a fully functional product
	- In the next iteration, the UX designers and developers evaluate the implemented functionalities to conclude how well the product works and if people like it
		- If something isn’t correct, the UX designers go back and make necessary changes to the design
		- modified design is evaluated again and implemented to complete the iteration
### UX Case Study Process
![[Pasted image 20260509100819.png|600]]
- UX designers can define various components to demonstrate the thought process and expertise
	- they might choose to include some or all of the following steps and components
	- decision depends on how much information they want to communicate about their process, insights, and the impact of their work
- Project Requirements (Define project requirements)
	- start off by defining the requirements of the project
	- provide the following details
		- selected project
		- Potential user challenges
		- Goals as a UX designer
- User Personas and Competitive Analysis (Research users and competition)
	- add information about the users and competition
	- includes
		- Data gathered from researching users
		- User personas
		- Insights from competitive analysis
- User Stories and Solution Sketches (Create user stories and solution sketches)
	- provide deeper insights into their design process
	- include the following details and artifacts
		- User stories
		- User journey and user flows
		- Solution sketches
- Wireframe (Draft a wireframe)
	- share rough outline of their design solution
	- include the following artifacts
		- Information architecture (IA) and sitemaps
		- Paper and digital wireframes
- Prototype (Prototype a solution)
	- use the wireframes and build on them
	- add the following artifacts
		- User interface (UI) design
		- Paper and digital prototypes
- Usability Testing and Results (Conduct usability testing and incorporate results)
	- more insights into their design process and its outcome
	- share the following details and artifacts
		- Usability testing plan
		- Test report and results
		- Revised prototypes
- Next Steps
	- might also provide the following information
		- Details of project team members
		- Methods to collaborate with developers
		- Design artifacts handed off to developers
		- Review feedback to refine the digital product
